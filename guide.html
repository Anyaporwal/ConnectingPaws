General First Aid Tips for All Animals

Stay calm and ensure your safety first. Gently restrain the animal. Stop bleeding using clean cloth or gauze. Do not feed or give water to unconscious animals. Always contact a vet as soon as possible.


1. Stay Calm and Safe

      Approach the injured animal slowly.
      Muzzle dogs if needed (injured animals may bite).
      Use a towel or cloth to gently restrain small animals or birds.

2. For Bleeding Wounds

      Apply gentle pressure with a clean cloth or gauze.
      Use antiseptic (betadine or diluted povidone-iodine) to clean around the wound.
      Bandage lightly to avoid cutting circulation.

3. For Burns

      Cool the area with cold (not ice) water for 5-10 minutes.
      Do not apply creams or oils.
      Cover with a clean, damp cloth and keep the animal calm.

4. For Breathing Issues or Choking

      Open the mouth carefully and check for visible obstruction.
      If needed, give gentle chest compressions.
      Seek immediate vet help if breathing doesn't normalize.

6. For Dehydration/Heatstroke

      Move animal to a shady or cool area.
      Offer water slowly.
      Dampen body with cool water, especially the paws and belly.

7. For Birds

      Place in a warm, quiet box with air holes.
      Avoid feeding immediately.
      Keep calm and consult a wildlife rehabber/vet.

First Aid for Dogs

Dogs are susceptible to various injuries, each requiring specific first aid measures. Below are some common types of injuries in dogs, along with corresponding YouTube videos demonstrating appropriate first aid techniques:


1. Bleeding Wounds

Accidents can lead to open wounds that bleed profusely. Applying direct pressure with a clean cloth can help control bleeding until veterinary care is available.




2. Shock

After a traumatic event, dogs may experience shock, indicated by symptoms like weakness, rapid breathing, and pale gums. Keeping the dog warm and calm is essential while seeking immediate veterinary attention.




3. Burn Injuries in Dogs

Burns in dogs can result from hot surfaces, scalding liquids, chemicals, or fire, leading to skin damage, pain, and risk of infection. Immediate first aid involves gently cooling the area with clean, cool water and seeking urgent veterinary care.




4. Emergency Transport of Injured Dog

The emergency handling of a dog suffering from trauma such as fractures, spinal injury, or internal bleeding. Proper immobilization and gentle transport are critical to prevent worsening the condition before veterinary care is available.


First Aid for Cats

Calm the cat first. Gently clean wounds and keep the cat warm. Contact a vet quickly.


1. Bleeding Wounds

Cats can suffer from lacerations or cuts due to accidents or fights. Apply gentle pressure with a clean cloth to control bleeding and avoid using antiseptics not meant for animals.



2. Burn Injuries in Cats

Burns in cats can result from hot surfaces, scalding liquids, chemicals, or fire, leading to skin damage, pain, and risk of infection. Immediate first aid involves gently cooling the area with clean, cool water and seeking urgent veterinary care.


First Aid for Birds

Commonly injured by kite strings, especially during festivals like Uttarayan. Handle with soft cloth. Place in a ventilated box. Treat visible wounds gently.


First Aid for Cattle

Clean wounds with diluted antiseptic. Cover with clean cloth. Avoid moving injured limbs.
